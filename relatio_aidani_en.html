HERE BEGINS
THE RELATION
OF BROTHER AIDAN,
MONK OF THE ISLE OF Ì

Foliated by Brother Bernard in the year of our Lord MCXLVII

Of His Wondrous Pilgrimage
Unto the Land Beyond the Western Sea
Wherein, It Is Said, the Eldar Dwell
Written in the Year of Our Lord DCCCXCVI

☩ ☩ ☩

I, Aidan, a sinner and unprofitable servant of our Lord Jesus Christ, an unworthy monk in the monastery which the blessed Columba founded upon the isle which we call in the Scottish tongue Ì, and in Latin the Isle of Columba, do write these things to the honour of God Almighty, who is to be adored in the Trinity, at the bidding of Brother Finan our most beloved abbot, lest the things which I have seen be given over to perpetual oblivion. Our brethren know that I am not a man of visions: never before hath an angel appeared unto me, never have I been caught up in ecstasy. But that which I am about to tell is true, so far as a man fallible and laden with sin can tell the truth before God, whose eye doth pierce all things.

☩ Of the author: Aidan, monk of Ì, disciple of Father Columba. Witness he alone — but God hath seen.

Many have asked me — and this I confess — wherefore I have not kept silence concerning these things. I answer: because by silence perchance I should sin against God, who hath deigned to reveal them unto me. What these wonders are in the scheme of divine providence, I dare not define: this I leave unto the doctors of the Church. I tell only what I have seen, what I have heard, what with mine own hand I have touched.

☩ Of the First Voyage ☩

It was the year from the Incarnation of the Lord DCCCXCII when first my heart began to kindle as with some flame, and in the night-prayer after Matins I would behold as in an image of the mind the western sea, and out of that sea a light arose that was not of the sun, nor of the moon, but of a third kind altogether. I told this unto Abbot Finan. He, a prudent man and well-versed in the Scriptures, made answer: If God doth call thee, tarry not. If the devil tempteth, prayer and fasting shall discern. And I fasted seven days, and prayed, and the vision did not depart but was confirmed. Then I understood that I must be called.

Cf. the Voyage of Abbot Brendan — he too sailed unto the West & saw wondrous things. Could it be the same land?

In the month of August, after the feast of Saint Columba, I went up into a small currach, made of wicker ribs and oxhides. Brother Cellach, beholding the harbour, made the sign of the cross over me and did not hide his tears. I sailed unto the West, leaving the Pole Star at my back. For three days a wondrous wind — neither too strong nor too languid, as though guided by the hand of some Power — filled my sails. Dolphins kept me company on the first day, on the second day birds I had not known, on the third day an absolute silence, and the sea became as a vast mirror, and the heaven and the deep seemed one.

☩ Of the Tempest and the Strange Light ☩

On the fourth day there arose a great tempest, and the waves rose up like mountains, and my little vessel — a wretched plank amid the vastness of the deep — was tossed as an autumn leaf in the wind. It was night, and a darkness without star. I prayed with a great voice and with the voice of my heart, calling upon God, and Saint Columba, and all the saints of Hibernia and Britain. And I made vows, that if I should come forth alive out of these waters, I would pass the remainder of my life in silence, fasting, and prayer, seeking no further morsel of human glory.

A note upon time: Four days and nights — as Jonah three days in the belly of the fish. A mystic number.

Then — suddenly, not by degrees — the tempest ceased. Not as tempests cease by nature, spending their force little by little, but as though some one had laid a hand upon the sea and said unto it: Peace, be still. The sea became as quiet as a forest pool at dawn. And I heard a voice — not from the outward air, but in the depth of my heart, like a light kindled in a cave — which said: Fear not, servant of God. Thou shalt see that which was prepared before the ages. Go whither the wind doth lead thee. And a new wind arose from the north, light and steady, and my little vessel of itself turned westward.

In the third watch of that night a light appeared upon the western horizon. It was not the light of the sun — for the sun was rising in the East — nor of the moon, which shone behind me, waning. It was a twofold light: the one silver, cold and pure as the first evening star multiplied a thousandfold; the other golden, warm and joyful as the summer sun at the sixth hour. And these two lights were not mingled, but shone apart, and between them was as it were a kind of choir, an alternation solemn and wondrous, so that the land before me was never in darkness, but ever abode in some light.

Here the hand of the scribe trembled — see the unsteady letters in the old exemplar.

☩ Of the Land of the Eldar ☩

At dawn my little vessel was brought into a most beautiful haven, with no oar of mine, as though some unseen hand held the stern. With trembling foot I stepped from the boat, and my foot touched the ground — and the grass beneath my feet was greener than any grass of Hibernia, or Britain, or any land mine eyes have seen. There were flowers whose names I know not, whose fragrance was as though all the flowers of the world were gathered into one. Trees most lofty, whose leaves shone like translucent gems even without the sun. And the very air — good God! — the very air was as the clearest water that thou shouldst drink and not be sated, but ever thirst the more.

A land without its own sun — cf. Apocalypse ch. XXI: the city hath no need of the sun neither of the moon. But that city is the heavenly one — this here seemeth earthly.

There was no sun in the heaven. This I marked first, and at first was afraid: the sky was pale and high and most pure, like the sky after a great summer rain, but the sun was absent. Yet there was light, neither of moon nor of sun — but some other, seemingly coming from a place afar that I could not yet see. Whence it flowed, that day I did not understand. Afterward I learnt — but of this, if God grant, I shall speak in its own place.

And there were mountains upon the horizon, high and snowy, the noblest I had ever seen — and in those mountains as it were white towers shone from afar, so many and so tall that at first I deemed them clouds shaped into the likeness of a city. But they were not clouds. They were buildings true, of white stone which itself seemed to bear its own light, raised by an art so great that no hand of man could equal.

Air which thou drinkest and the thirst doth grow — who doth not call to mind the living water whereof the Lord speaketh (Jn. IV)? But here it is not water but air, and the thirst is not lessened but increased. A wondrous gift of Aman — as though thirst itself were blessedness, after Mt. V, 6.

And whilst I stood thus, as it were set outside myself, knowing not whether I waked or were in a vision, lo, a certain man of the inhabitants of that land — whom I knew not yet by name nor by kindred — silently stood at my side. I heard not his feet coming upon the grass; I saw only his shadow beside mine in that light which was without sun. He asked not my name, he questioned not whence I came; only with a gracious sign of the head he bade me follow. And I followed without fear — for in his countenance there was nothing that might stir fear, but rather that quiet which the elder brethren are wont to have in long prayer.

☩ Of the Twofold Light upon the Mountain ☩

When little by little mine eyes had grown accustomed to the light of that land, upon a mountain afar off beyond the city I marked two fountains of light, from which — as it then seemed to me — all the light of the region flowed. Far they were, perhaps a day's journey or more, and I beheld them not as things with their own form, but as two glittering thrones set upon a high mountain: the one of pure silver, like the moon on a frosty night, the other of warm gold, like the sun in the month of August. And they shone by turns: when one languished, the other waxed; and when at times both shone together, such was the beauty that tears came to me without any cause known.

Two trees giving light without a sun — do they perhaps preserve a memory of Paradise before the fall? In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth — and before the sun was made, there was light. Gen. I.

And because I am a monk and accustomed to name all things by the words of Scripture and creation, I called them within myself two great luminaries, or two Trees of Light, although from such a distance I knew not whether they were truly trees, or pillars of light, or towers, or some other thing I knew not yet. For as he who from afar beholdeth a vessel upon the sea saith «a vessel», even though it might perchance be an isle, or a cloud, or some other thing.

But my companion — who had ascended with me from the shore in silence, and whose name and kindred I yet knew not — beholding whither mine eyes were turned, with a voice soft and grave said:

A wounded light thou seest, foreign guest. The first Enemy smote it of old — whereof I will not speak this day. But the true light ever drives away the darkness, though otherwise than before. Behold, and be not deceived.

Let the reader not doubt this: he who is made of the Song understandeth the song.

I heard his words as in a dream. Yet their full sense I did not then grasp: who that Enemy first might be, of whom he willed to keep silence, I knew not at all, nor durst I question of mine own judgement. But his voice bore so great an authority that I doubted not that what he said was true, and I kept these words within my heart as a hidden seed, that in due season should germinate. For a man who first cometh into a strange place ought first to be silent and to listen.

The remainder of that day I gazed from afar upon that mountain and the alternation of the lights between silver and gold, and it was unto me as a long silent prayer, even though my words were not in it. And when the light — if it be lawful so to speak of those that are not our sun — began to languish and the evening hour drew near, that companion said unto me: Enough for this day. Let us enter the city. And we entered.

What I saw on that day from afar I have not forgotten, nor was I able to forget; but what it truly was, this I learned afterward, and in their proper places in this little book I shall write — both that which pertaineth unto mine eyes, and that which pertaineth unto the heart.

What are these trees in the order of creation? Could they be the tree of life whereof in Genesis? But that was in Eden, not beyond the ocean…

☩ Of the Inhabitants and Their Visage ☩

The inhabitants of that land — whom I afterward understood to be those who name themselves Eldar, that is in Latin as it were They Who First Saw the Stars, because they were born before the sun and the moon, beneath the light of the primordial stars — were in form like unto man, but as much excelling as man can be conceived to be excelled.

Question: bodily angels? — They seem not. Pre-Adamites? — But where in Scripture? A different species created by God, beyond our understanding. To be feared and to adore the God who hath made such things.

Their stature was tall, as of well-formed men, but all in some manner majestic and serene, as though none of them had ever feared, or laboured overmuch, or been sick. Their faces shone — not only as the face of a healthy and joyful man is wont to shine, but with some inward light, as crystal before a candle, in which the very candle is in some manner seen. Their hair was as gold or living silver, not in colour, but as though that very matter were alive in their hair. And their eyes — O Lord God! — their eyes were as wells without bottom, in which thou couldst see as it were the memory of all things that have been done from the beginning of the world, and stars, and songs that no human ear doth hear.

There was nothing in them deformed, nothing foul, nothing mortal to behold. Their garments were white and grey and silver, nowhere torn nor soiled. And what most wondrous seemed: in their faces fear or sadness could not be seen — not because they knew them not, but because they were as it were beyond them, like one who knoweth sorrow well and yet, emerging from it, now standeth higher. Afterward he himself — of whom I shall presently speak, and his name — explained unto me: We do not live in time as ye do. We are mindful of all that hath been. This is both a gift and a heavy burden.

Their tongue — O what a wondrous tongue! — was as music made of words, or words made of music: every voice resounded as a harp-string struck, and when many spake together it was as a choir of singers in the night of the Lord's Nativity. I spake in Hibernian, and in Latin, and a little in Saxon, which I had learnt from our English brethren in the monastery. And then — not without astonishment — I recognized in their midst that very one who had silently led me to the city: and he was elder among them, not in stature, but in a certain splendour as of a halo about him, which I had not seen on the way, as though only then for the first time had he revealed himself. He made a sign of silence to the others and began to speak unto me in a new tongue.

And wonder of wonders, and grace of God: I understood him. Not as a stranger who after many months learns a barbarous tongue little by little, but as in a dream: the meaning came into my mind without labour, without the rumour of words, as though his voice spake directly into my heart, with words as it were pierced through with light.

Come, said he, thou art a guest in Aman. To not many sons of Death is it given to come hither. Who hath sent thee?

I made answer, almost bending the knee: My God, the Maker of heaven and earth, who is adored in three persons, and Saint Columba my patron, who sent him upon this way.

And his countenance became as the sun emerging from long clouds — joy and sadness together, marvellously mingled, and his eyes — I have already said that their eyes were deep — moved as deep waters at these words. And he said with a low voice, as though uttering a sacred name in the holy of holies: Ilúvatar.

Exceeding fair — so also are the angels when they appear. But angels do not eat, whereas these eat and laugh. Which is wondrous.

☩ Of the Discourse with Finrod ☩

His name — so far as I was able to render it from their tongue into Latin — was Findarato or Finrod: Golden End it seemed to mean, or Crowned with wisdom and gold. Among his own he was as a king or priest, not by violence or fear, but because all loved him and desired to speak with him. He himself said that he was of a kindred which calls itself Noldor — that is, as he interpreted, They Who Know Many Things or Sons of Wisdom.

Finrod — name rendered in Latin from their own tongue. It signifieth as it were Golden Wisdom or the Crown of Gold and Wisdom. A magnificent man.

He led me into the city which I had seen from my little boat — and greater it was than from afar I had believed. It was built upon a mountain of white stone shining with its own light, with towers so many and so high that they almost touched the clouds. The streets were paved with stone polished and smooth. Through the middle of the street ran a singing brook, and trees grew in the very street as in a vast garden. The whole people gazed upon us, not in hostility but with a merciful curiosity: for I was — I understood afterward — as a bird of strange kind among them, a curious thing, tender, fragile, mortal.

In a great hall — wholly built of crystal and marble, in whose summit a certain soft light entered through vast windows, whose origin I then knew not — we sat, myself and Finrod, and held long discourses for hours which I could not number, for the hours there flow otherwise.

A white city upon a mountain — cf. the city in the Apocalypse: descending from heaven from God, having the glory of God (Apoc. XXI, 10). But this hath not yet descended — it is already there.

Finrod told me of the origin of the world. He told of the Great Song — which I understood to be the Creation — when Ilúvatar himself in the beginning sang and bade his Powers — Ainur he called them, whom I understood to be the Greater Angels — sing with him melodies ineffable. And they sang, and out of their song and the will of Ilúvatar the world was fashioned — not as a potter shapes a vessel, but as a singer creates a harmony: from the very sound, from the very beauty.

But one of the Greater Angels — whom they named Melkor, and whom I understood beyond doubt to be the Adversary, whom we call Satan — willed to sing against the harmony, and sowed discord in the song. And when I heard this name Melkor, suddenly there came back to my memory what some days before beside the mountain of lights I had heard from my companion: of the first Enemy of whom he had then willed to keep silence. I understood therefore that he was the same: the voice which corrupted the first harmony, and the hand — as shall presently be made manifest — which afterward slew the lights of the world. One is the hand, one the sin, though its works be many and divers through the ages.

Light before the sun — as is said in Genesis: God said, Let there be light. And there was light. This was on the first day. The sun, on the fourth. What lay between? Scripture is silent.

☩ And Ilúvatar did not annihilate that discord, but received it into himself, and out of it drew forth a beauty yet more wondrous — as certain shadows in a painting make the surrounding light shine the brighter. Pondering this, I was astonished: even in the theology of these Immortals the same mystery which we venerate in the Providence of God! ☩

Creation through song — wondrous! In the Psalms David praises God with song, the angels sing Glory in the highest; but here by the very song the world was made? A wondrous theology — yet not impious.

Then Finrod, seeing me attentive, took on a voice as of one singing — for something doth alter in the voice of the Eldar when they speak of the most ancient things — and began to tell of the first lights of the world, before the sun and the moon were made. Which narration so moved me that thereafter I could not sleep that night, and even today, when I recall it, I feel as though some window in my heart had been opened that I cannot close.

Of old, said he, when the world was young and the sun was not yet set in the heaven, the Valar — whom ye might call Powers — planted in our land two Trees, to give light unto the dwellers of Aman. They were not trees as those which thou seest in this garden: they were as the very light contracted into the form of a tree, as the Wisdom of Ilúvatar manifesting itself through root, and frond, and flower.

These two Trees I myself drew, as Father Cellach had described to me that he had seen them in the ancient codex made by Aidan's hand, before time should efface them. — brother Coeman

And he described them with a voice so clear that I almost saw them with mine eyes: the First Tree, which they called Telperion, great and most ancient, having green leaves and silver flowers, from whose flowers there flowed a light pure and cool as the full moon, but without shadow and without cessation. Its name, said he, I might perchance render into thy speech as Silver Foliage or the Splendour of Silver. Its roots — they say — held all the land of Aman beneath them, and when light winds moved its branches, the very light as it were sang, glittering among the leaves. This was the first light of the world.

The Second Tree, said he, somewhat smaller but more ardent, had golden flowers from which there flowed a light warm and joyful, like the summer sun but without harmful heat — a light pure and benign, beneath which all things appeared more beautiful than they are. Her Laurelin we named, that is as it were Singing Gold or Golden Melody. For the light of that tree was as music: not such as thou hearest with the ear, but such as the eye and the whole body do feel. By turns they shone, so that the silver light little by little waned while the golden waxed, and again the golden was diminished and the silver flowered — and in a short space of time both shone together, in a mingling of light so precious that even now, as I speak of this, tears come unto me, though many years have passed.

And he was silent a little while, and I saw his eyes grow moist — which among the Eldar is a thing most rare. Then, with voice changed, he continued: But that Melkor of whom I have just spoken to thee, whose discord was heard in the Song, afterward — when he had already been cast forth and had come again into the world — pursued those Trees with hatred. For their light accused all his dark work. And he took counsel with a certain monstrous creature — whose name I am not glad to utter in the daylight, lest haply she should attend — which drank darkness as others drink water, and was so ancient that not even the Valar know whence she came. These two together, Melkor and that beast of the darkness, came on a certain festival morn unto the Trees, and wounded them, and that power of darkness sucked out all their light and life.

Melkor = Satan? Another name, the same substance: the first angel who willed against the harmony of God, and fell through pride (Is. XIV). The thing itself is one, only the name is other.

True night then first fell upon Aman. That which we had not before known: that the light could be slain. The Valar wept as no creature shall ever weep in this world, for they themselves had made the Trees with their own hand and had as it were laid their hearts within them. And we — Eldar — who under that light had been born and under it had lived so many ages, stood mute, knowing not what was true darkness. Many things flowed from that time whereof it is not fitting that thou shouldst hear today: exile, the sword, brother against brother, and a long and bitter labour. This only know: the first light of the world was slain.

And when he had said these things, I, pondering in my heart on the death of Christ — on the light of the world which was extinguished on Golgotha — kept silence, and could not answer him. How should it be said unto him? For neither did I then know how to say it. But I wrote it in my memory, as Mary wrote the words of the shepherds in her heart, as a thing which God shall some day expound.

Yet a certain thing, said Finrod afterward, as though perceiving the sense of my soul, hath survived from the Trees, and before thy return thou shalt see — if it be pleasing unto thee. Not those very things which were: for those have perished, as I have said. But what surviveth — is a memory, which Ilúvatar suffereth not utterly to perish. For what truly was beautiful is in some manner preserved with him, though not in the same manner as it was before. But of this we shall speak when the time cometh.

And I understood that I had yet some great thing to see, and this word — what truly was beautiful is preserved with him — was in my heart as a seed laid down, that should afterward give its flowers.

Tears of the Eldar — a wondrous thing. Not such as ours, from the pain of the flesh; but from the memory of things which they hold and are constrained to hold. A heavy burden.

☩ Of Their Immortality and Their Lot ☩

I asked Finrod concerning their state after death. And here again I saw in his countenance something as of a passing cloud — not that sadness which I had marked on the previous day when he spake of the Trees, but another: a deep questioning, as a man who looks into a mirror and sees himself and not himself, and marvels both ways.

This place is most theologically difficult. If they die not in sin, do they not need redemption? But Augustine teacheth that every creature is affected after the fall of Adam. How then these?

We do not die as ye die, said he slowly, as though weighing his words. Our body may perish in war or in a grievous wound. But our spirit — that which ye call the soul — abideth for ages within this world, to be summoned to a new flesh when the time shall come. We are bound to this Song of Creation, to this world, even unto its uttermost end. A pause. But ye — sons of Death, sons of the Unknown Gift — burn as a brief flame, and after death ye go on unto an unknown way. Whither ye go, Ilúvatar hath kept unto himself. Unto us he hath not revealed it. And this is, and here in a wondrous manner he used a voice as if speaking of a thing precious and almost to be envied, a gift given unto you which is denied unto us.

Long did I dwell upon these words. A gift — death, a gift? We who all our life resist death, who have wept the deaths of parents and friends and brethren, who in the last fear ask for the viaticum — we have death as a gift? And these Immortals, unto whom we seem as wretched and brief and bound to perish, marvel at us by reason of that very brevity and mortality?

Finrod saw my thought in my face and smiled — and his smile was as a dawn: Misinterpret not this. We envy you not, we despise you not. But after so many ages we have seen many of your kind — few, very few, who have come hither — and in all of you there is a certain light which is not in us: the light of brief but ardent things. Ye love more intensely because ye lose quickly. Ye sing more strongly because your song knoweth how to end. In this ye are like unto a flame — and the flame is brighter than the star, though the star liveth longer.

Death as a gift! A wondrous paradox. Could these Immortals envy our death? What is freedom from the world — but death itself?

☩ Of the Preaching and the Wondrous Reply ☩

Then — and this, brethren, I did out of charity and of mine office, not out of arrogance — I told Finrod those things which we know and believe and preach: of God the Father who so loved the world; of the eternal Son who took on our human flesh — wondrous and astounding! — and in that flesh suffered and died and on the third day rose again; of the Holy Ghost who quickeneth the Church. I told how through this passion and resurrection the eternal gate hath been opened to men unto life.

Here is the Gordian knot of the whole narration. If the Eldar knew Ilúvatar before Christ — what of them after Christ? Have they salvation? The Fathers of the Church have not treated of this.

And Finrod listened to me — and this also was wondrous — not only lending his ears but with all his body, with all his mind, as a man who at last hears from the first source a long history told by many. And when I named the Son of God descending into flesh and dying upon the cross, his countenance became as the sky when heavy clouds pass over the sun: a shadow, but not darkness.

When I had finished speaking, he was silent long. So long that I thought I had offended him. But there was no anger in his silence — there was a deep consideration, as one who in his soul setteth a long reckoning. At last he said:

Of this One who descended into death that he might conquer death — and who was born of a mortal woman and bore in flesh all the sorrow of the flesh — we have had among us ancient prophecies, voices as it were heard in vision, concerning which not all agreed nor were all clear. Now I understand better. But this way — as thou sayest — is the way of the sons of Death, the way which receiveth the Unknown Gift. We are not sons of Death. Our way is otherwise — and whether we have a way, or have walked a way, or whether we also need a hand to lead us — this we seek and find not, and in this questioning we are age upon age.

And Finrod arose and went unto the window where the silver light entered and made his face silver, and he spake the last words which I shall write of him in this book:

Thou hast the sin of one man in the history of thy flesh. We have the sin of one Angel in the history of our memory. Perhaps both stand in need of mercy — but a mercy other and other, as the wounds are other and other. That distinction Ilúvatar alone knoweth. We serve. We love. We create. We keep. We sing. Whether it suffice — is in his hand, not in mine.

And I could not answer him. Nor today still can I.

Prophecy among the Eldar — how, and from whom, given? If they knew of the Incarnation, hath the Spirit of God spoken also unto them? A great questioning.

☩ Of the Memory of the Light ☩

When at last the day came on which I must return — not because Finrod willed to cast me out, but because he himself had said unto me that my time in that land was ending, lest roots of yet another kind grow within me — he arose at the dawn of that day, and called me from the place where I slept. And he spake with a soft voice, as one wishing to lead a friend unto some secret: Come with me. One thing remaineth for thee before I let thee go. Follow, and be silent.

Father Cellach once told me of a monk who saw the light of God in silence and kept silence all his life. Now I believe this can be. — brother Coeman

And I followed him through the streets of the city which I had not yet trod, through gardens still closed before the full light, through the western gate which the warden opened unto us without words, as one who had awaited us. We went out of the city and ascended a hill not high but set apart from the walls, on which a few customary trees stood, and such silence was there that I could hear my blood in mine ears. And as we ascended, suddenly there came into my mind: this very hill I had seen from afar on my first day, when I yet knew not what it was. Then had I called it the mount of the lights, and had beheld sparks of silver and gold upon its summit. Now I came unto that which my eye had then apprehended from afar, but my mind had not understood.

On the summit of the hill there was no building, nor great tree, nor monument made by hand — but the very space was sacred, as a church without walls, and the earth there seemed higher and truer than the rest of the earth. Finrod said nothing, but stood beside me with bowed head, as one who prayed in a most holy place.

A secret hill — cf. Song of Songs IV, 12: a garden enclosed, a fountain sealed. A place kept by God, it seemeth.

And little by little, with no sign that I might see, from the very earth around our feet a light began to emerge — not strong as the sun nor plainly lunar, but most fine, silver at first, afterward golden, afterward both mingling themselves. It was no shining flower, no visible tree, no fountain — the light itself, without visible source, flowed forth from the very earth, imprinting forms in the air as though it sang a silent music to the eyes.

And I stood and gazed, and my tears flowed without my will. I saw not the Trees themselves — for they were not. But I saw what survived of them, the testimony of that which had been wounded and had not utterly perished. A certain like sweetness was in those lights such as I had once seen in the face of a man who had risen from a grievous sickness and beheld the sun again for the first time: that face is not what it was before the sickness, but something higher doth appear in it — it is a face that hath seen death and yet liveth. So unto me was this light.

A light without visible source — as the Spirit who breatheth where he will, whose voice thou hearest, but knowest not whence he cometh (Jn. III, 8). The same reason.

How long we stood there, I know not. Finrod was silent, and I was silent, and the hours there were what they were. When at last the light began little by little to wane, as though the day itself were leaving the hill, Finrod said with a voice more moved than I had heard on any other day: So it is. What truly was beautiful, Ilúvatar suffereth not utterly to perish. More this day I shall not say.

While those words yet hung in the air and the light was little by little waning, I cast mine eyes down upon the earth — and beside my foot I saw a small stone which I had not before noted, white as fresh snow, scarce the size of a hazelnut, in which a certain thin but steady light abode. I bent and lifted it, scarcely thinking what I did. Finrod silently watched me, neither did he himself bend down to the earth, nor did he seek any stone.

When the stone was already in my palm, he said with a humble voice: Such stones are sometimes found here on the hill, rarely, and none of ours do gather them. Perhaps they are a memory of greater ones than we, whom this day I dare not name. What hath come to thee hath not come by chance: take it, and bear it with thee across the sea. But beware of one thing: hide it not unto thyself alone, neither set it in a silver vessel as though it were thine own treasure. He who hideth his light in darkness himself goeth astray — which hath even befallen the greatest of us at times.

And he laid his hand upon my shoulder — a light weight, as of a bird — and we descended by the same way, full of silence, and I knew that something within my heart had been changed by what I had seen. What precisely had been changed, I knew not yet then. But from that day to this, this vision ceaseth not to return in my dreams:

☩ Of the Return and the Viaticum ☩

After the vision of that hill — as though I had been seven days in that land, or as much as seven days seemed to me to be, for time there flows otherwise, gently and abundantly as honey poured from a bowl rather than water through a torrent — Finrod went with me to the harbour where my little vessel awaited. I saw in it certain provisions which I had not laid: bread that was not corrupted, and water in a small vessel. Some unseen hand had provided.

Seven days there, the last day the Memory of the Light — a perfect number. Three years without? Like the Hibernian tales of the Faerie land — he who entereth, going forth hath lost his time. To be wary.

Then, before I went up into the boat, I drew that stone from my scrip and looked upon it again — and it seemed to me other than upon the hill: it was translucent, as alpine crystal, and the light within it was no longer thin and solitary as before, but composite: not warm as gold, not cold as the moon, but as it were a mixture of both in a point of least repose. Finrod stood beside me silent, and through his eyes there passed then a certain longing more ancient than all the days — as the memory of things which no mortal hath touched. And with a moved voice he said: Give not unto oblivion what thou hast seen here. Not many of thy kind shall we see further — the time shall come — but the memory of thee we shall hold within us.

That stone — which is now unto me, among all things that I possess, the most precious — I keep in my most secret scrip, beneath my habit. By night I sometimes look upon it: there is light within. I know not what it is. I know not whether it be a miracle or some unknown nature. Committing it to God, I hold it and keep silence.

A jewel left by Aidan in the reliquary of the monastery. I saw it in the year of our Lord CMXV. There is light within. A testimony. brother Coeman

I sailed. And after three days — or three years, for the brethren afterward told me that I had been absent for a triennium, which I did not believe until I saw the calendar — I came into known waters, and recognized the seals of Hibernia, and the birds of Hibernia, and at last beheld the cliff of Hibernia in the East. And in the harbour which is near unto our monastery I put in, and Brother Cellach, who had wept me as gone three years, now laughed and wept at once at the gate.

After he returned, I saw him in few days. Otherwise he spake. Otherwise he kept silence. Otherwise he gazed. Where art thou now, brother Aidan? — brother Cellach

I was absent for three years. Unto me they seemed as three days. Time in the land of Aman floweth otherwise. This among other things I leave to the judgement of God.

☩ Conclusion and Confession ☩

☩ ☩ ☩

These things are true, so far as a man fallible and weighed down with many sins is able to tell the truth. To God and his saints, and to the Church our mother, I leave it to be judged what those Eldar are in the scheme of divine providence, and what is to be hoped or feared concerning them. I am not Augustine. I am not Jerome. I am a fisherman and a poor little monk who went up into his currach and returned changed.

When I first read this, I deemed it either a fiction or a deliriment. But the jewel is a witness. Abbot Finan

This only do I say, brethren, and lay it up in your heart: In the face of Finrod I saw more of true goodness than in the faces of many men who call themselves Christians and approach unto the Eucharist but hate their neighbour. In his words concerning Ilúvatar — whom we acknowledge to be God the Father — I heard more true reverence than in many sermons which are preached from cathedrae throughout the Church and quickly slip from the ears. In their questioning of the way of their salvation — in that deep and noble ignorance — I saw more humility and honesty than in many who think they know all things and seek no further way because they believe themselves already to have arrived.

☩ Whether the Eldar shall be saved — this I ask and answer not. Whether they desire baptism — I know not, for I knew not how to speak unto them rightly thereof. Whether Christ suffered for them — perhaps, for God hath of his goodness somewhat for every creature. But let the theologians treat of these things — I only bear witness. ☩

One thing I add — not that I might answer what I cannot answer, but because keep silence on this also I cannot. When upon the hill on the last day I stood, I saw: that which the Enemy had slain, God did not suffer utterly to perish. The light upon the earth still abode. I know not what this meaneth concerning the lot of those who kept that light. But if God doth not forget the wounded light, perhaps he forgetteth not those also who loved it. This I teach not; this I hope.

Pray for me, brother Aidan a sinner, and pray — if it be lawful — for those Immortals beyond the ocean, whom God himself knoweth in what manner and how much, and who — this I know for certain — love their God Ilúvatar and serve him faithfully in their land, and sing songs whereof this world doth not know, and keep the memory of things from the beginning of the world, that it perish not.

☩ Have mercy upon us all, O Lord, and judge us mercifully — both knowing and unknowing, mortal and immortal, sons of the Gift and sons of the Song. ☩
