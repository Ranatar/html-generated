Illuminated by Brother Bernard in the year of Our Lord MCXLVII

Of His Wondrous Pilgrimage
Unto the Land Beyond the Western Sea
Wherein the Eldar Are Said to Dwell
Written in the Year of Our Lord DCCCXCVI

☩ ☩ ☩

I, Aidan, a sinner and unprofitable servant of our Lord Jesus Christ, an unworthy monk in the monastery which blessed Columba founded upon the isle that we call in the Scottish tongue Ì and in Latin Insula Columbae, do write these things unto the honour of Almighty God, who is to be adored in the Trinity, at the bidding of our most beloved abbot brother Finan, lest these things which I have seen should be given over unto perpetual oblivion. Our brethren know that I am no man of visions: never aforetime hath an angel appeared unto me, nor have I ever been lifted up in any trance. Yet that which I am about to set forth is true, in so far as a fallible man laden with sins is able to declare the truth before God, whose eye doth pierce all things.

☩ Concerning the author: Aidan, monk of Ì, disciple of Father Columba. Witness himself alone — but God did see.

Many besought me — and this I do confess — wherefore I had not kept silence touching these matters. I make answer: because by silence I might haply sin against God, who hath vouchsafed to shew these things unto me. What these wonders may be in the scheme of divine providence, I dare not myself define: that I leave unto the doctors of the Church. I do but tell that which I saw, that which I heard, that which with mine own hand I touched.

☩ Of the First Voyage ☩

It was the year of the Incarnation of the Lord DCCCXCII when first my heart began to be enkindled as with a certain flame, and in nightly prayer after Matins I beheld, as it were in an image of the mind, the western sea, and out of that sea a light did arise, neither of the sun nor of the moon, but of a third kind altogether. This I told unto Finan the abbot. He, a prudent man and well exercised in the Scriptures, made answer: If God doth call thee, tarry not. If the devil doth tempt, prayer and fasting shall discern. And I fasted seven days, and I prayed, and the vision did not depart but was confirmed. Then I understood that I was being called.

Cf. Navigatio Brendani abbatis — and he too sailed unto the West & beheld great wonders. Belike it is the same land?

In the month of August, after the feast of Saint Columba, I went aboard a little curach, fashioned of withy ribs and ox-hides. Brother Cellach, beholding from the harbour, made the sign of the cross over me and could not hide his tears. I sailed westward, leaving the Pole-star behind me. For three days a wondrous wind — neither too violent nor too faint, as though guided by the hand of some Power — filled my sails. Dolphins did keep me company upon the first day; upon the second day, birds such as I knew not; upon the third day, an utter silence, and the sea was become as a vast looking-glass, and heaven and the deep did seem to be one.

☩ Of the Tempest and the Unwonted Light ☩

On the fourth day there arose a great tempest, and the waves did rear themselves up like mountains, and my skiff — a wretched plank amid the vastness of the deep — was tossed as an autumn leaf upon the wind. It was night, and a darkness without star. I prayed with a loud voice and with the voice of my heart, calling upon God and upon holy Columba and upon all the saints of Hibernia and Britannia. And I made vows, that if I should come forth alive out of these waters, the remainder of my life I would spend in silence and fasting and prayer, seeking no more any morsel of human glory.

A note upon time: four days and nights — even as Jonah's three in the belly of the fish. A mystical number.

Then — of a sudden, and not by degrees — the tempest ceased. Not as tempests do naturally cease, spending their force little by little, but as though some one had laid his hand upon the sea and said unto it: Peace, be still. The sea was become as still as a forest pool at dawn. And I heard a voice — not in the outward air, but in the deep of my heart, as a light kindled in a cavern — which said: Fear not, O servant of God. Thou shalt see that which was prepared before the ages. Go thou whither the wind doth bear thee. And a new wind arose out of the north, light and steadfast, and my skiff did turn of itself unto the West.

In the third watch of that night a light appeared upon the western horizon. It was not the light of the sun — for the sun was rising in the East — nor of the moon, which behind me shone in her waning. It was a twofold light: the one of silver, cold and pure as the first evening star multiplied a thousandfold; the other of gold, warm and joyful as a summer sun at the sixth hour. And these two lights were not confounded together, but did shine apart, and between them there was as it were a certain chorus, an alternation solemn and wondrous, so that the land before me was never in darkness, but did ever abide in some light or other.

In this place the hand of the scribe did tremble — see the troubled letters in the old exemplar.

☩ Of the Land of the Eldar ☩

At the dawn my skiff was brought into a certain most fair haven, with no oar of mine, as though an unseen hand did hold the stern. With trembling foot I came forth from the skiff, and my foot did touch the ground — and the grass beneath my feet was greener than any grass of Hibernia or Britannia or any land that ever mine eyes have beheld. There were flowers whose names I know not, whose fragrance was as though all the flowers of the world had been gathered into one. Trees most lofty, whose leaves did shine like translucent gems even without the sun. And the air itself — good God! — the air itself was as though thou shouldst drink most clear water and not be satisfied, but ever more shouldst thirst.

A land with no sun of its own — cf. Apocalypse cap. XXI: the city hath no need of the sun, neither of the moon. But that city is heavenly — yet this doth seem to be earthly.

There was no sun in the heaven. This first I marked, and at first I was afraid: the heaven was pale and high and most pure, as the heaven after a great summer rain, yet the sun was wanting. Yet there was a light, neither of moon nor of sun — but of some other kind, as it were coming from a place afar off which I could not yet behold. Whence it did flow, that day I understood not. Afterward I learned — but of this, if God shall grant it, I shall speak in its own place.

And there were mountains upon the horizon, lofty and snow-clad, the most noble that ever I had beheld — and in those mountains there did shine, as it were, white towers from afar off, so many and so lofty that at first I supposed them to be clouds fashioned into the likeness of a city. But they were not clouds. They were true buildings, of white stone that did seem to bear its own light, builded with such art as no hand of man could equal.

An air whereof one doth drink, and his thirst groweth the more — who doth not call to mind the living water whereof the Lord doth speak (John IV)? But here it is not water but air, and the thirst is not lessened but increased. A wondrous gift of Aman — as though thirst itself were blessedness, after Matt. V, 6.

And whilst I stood thus, as one placed beside himself, neither knowing whether I waked or were in a vision, behold, a certain man of the dwellers of that land — whom I knew not yet by name nor by kindred — did silently take his stand at my side. I heard not his feet come over the grass; I saw only his shadow beside mine in that light which was without a sun. He asked not my name, nor did he inquire whence I came; only by a benign motion of the head he bade me follow. And I followed without fear — for in his countenance there was nothing that should stir up fear, but rather that quietness which our aged brethren are wont to bear after long prayer.

☩ Of the Twofold Light upon the Mountain ☩

When little by little mine eyes had accustomed themselves unto the light of that land, upon a mountain afar beyond the city I marked two fountains of light, whence — so it seemed unto me then — all the light of the region did flow. Far they were, peradventure a day's journey or more, and I beheld them not as things bearing a proper form, but as two glittering seats set upon a high mountain: the one of pure silver, as the moon upon a frosty night; the other of warm gold, as the sun in the month of August. And by turns they shone: when one waxed faint, the other grew; and when at times both did blaze together, so great was the beauty that tears came unto me without any known cause.

Two trees that give light without a sun — peradventure they keep some memory of Paradise before the Fall? In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth — and before the sun was made, there was light. Gen. I.

And inasmuch as I am a monk and accustomed to name all things by the words of Scripture and of created things, I called them in mine own thought two great luminaries, or two Trees of Light, albeit from so great a distance I knew not whether they were truly trees or pillars of light or towers or some other thing which I knew not yet. For as he that beholdeth a ship afar in the sea doth call it a ship, even though peradventure it be an island or a cloud or some other thing.

But my companion — who had ascended with me from the shore in silence, and whose name and kindred I knew not as yet — perceiving whither mine eyes were turned, said with a soft and grave voice:

A wounded light dost thou behold, O pilgrim guest. The First Enemy did smite it of old — of whom this day I shall not speak. Yet the true light doth ever drive back the darkness, albeit otherwise than aforetime. Look, and deceive not thyself.

Let the reader not doubt of this: he that is made out of the Song doth understand the song.

I heard his words as though in a dream. Yet the meaning of them I did not then wholly apprehend: who that First Enemy might be, of whom he would keep silence, I knew not at all, neither did I dare to inquire of mine own will. But his voice had so great authority that I doubted not the truth of what he said, and I kept these words in my heart as a hidden seed, that in its own time it might spring forth. For he that cometh first into a strange place ought first to keep silence and to hearken.

The remainder of that day I gazed upon that mountain afar off, and upon the alternation of the lights between silver and gold, and this was unto me as a long silent prayer, even though my words were not in it. And when the light — if it be lawful so to speak of those things which are not our sun — had begun to grow faint, and the evening hour drew nigh, that companion said unto me: It sufficeth for to-day. Let us go in unto the city. And we went in.

That which I beheld that day from afar, I have not forgotten, neither could I forget it; but what it was in truth, this thereafter I learned, and in their proper places in this little book I shall write — both that which pertaineth unto mine eyes, and that which pertaineth unto the heart.

What are these trees in the order of creation? Could it be the tree of life whereof we read in Genesis? But that was in Eden, not beyond the ocean…

☩ Of the First Night and the Vigil ☩

That companion which silently had led me from the shore unto the city did bring me into a certain cell within the walls, simple and clean, wherein were only a couch and a board and one window opened toward the east, and with a nod of his head he departed and left me alone. That cell was somewhat like the cell of any of our own brethren — yet the very stone seemed cleaner and lighter, and the silence was such that I heard mine own blood in mine ears. And when, weary, I let myself down upon the couch, yet I could not take any sleep.

Try the spirits whether they be of God (1 John IV, 1). Our brother did rightly — as the Fathers have taught — to discern every tempting spirit by the sign of the cross and by the name of Jesus.

For there did suddenly arise in my mind a grave thought, and one well known unto monks: whether perchance I were not in the hand of God in this land, but in some subtle snare of the devils, this long time aforehand prepared. For I remembered the word of blessed Paul unto the Corinthians: Satan himself is transformed into an angel of light; and my heart remembered also the readings of the Egyptian Fathers, who under the show of angels did oft find the devil, and only by the sign of the cross and by the invocation of the name of Jesus did put him to flight. I rose therefore from the bed, and standing in the midst of the cell I made the sign of the holy cross unto the four quarters of the world, with this prayer or the like: If it be God which hath sent me hither, may He keep me; but if the devil do go about to work anything here, may he be cast back in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ crucified and risen again, Amen. And I called upon Columba our father, and upon all the saints of Hibernia and of Britannia whose names came unto my remembrance. And little by little — not on a sudden but as it were dew descending upon the grass — I began to feel peace within my heart: not for that I was sure that these were angels or men or anything else, but for that I was sure that my God was present unto me even in this land, and this was enough for an honest sleep.

Yet, since mine eyes would still not close, I drew nigh unto the window which looked toward the east. The air of the night was thin and pure, as the air of January nights upon our mountain, but more temperate. And I lifted up mine eyes unto the heavens: and blessed be God, the stars there were the same. That Wain of seven stars which we are first taught as children to know, did stand in his place, a little turned but knowable as the face of a friend after a long absence. And that star which they call the Pole-star, the axis of all heaven, was held in his height immovable. The other constellations also, whose names I had either learned or which without name I did remember, kept their places. And this above all other things was unto me a solace: we are beneath the same heaven, therefore beneath the same God; for though some sea did lie between me and Hibernia, and an unknown land beneath my feet, yet the heaven was one, and He which created that heaven did behold me here as He beheld me there.

The heavens declare the glory of God (Ps. XVIII). Whatsoever land a man may tread — the heaven above him is one, and He that speaketh in heaven doth speak everywhere.

But when I had long looked upon the stars, my heart did mark one wondrous thing: the moon I beheld nowhere. For I looked for her after the wont of all nights, and there was none rising from the eastern part, none yet standing in the heaven, none anywhere. I called to mind then that waning moon which had shone behind me in the tempest: if so many days had gone by since the tempest, by this time the moon should be nigh unto new, and peradventure was already in the heaven yet so thin that the eye could not catch her. And there did rise in my mind a certain thought, slowly waxing: if neither the sun was in this heaven when first I came, nor doth the moon now appear, what manner of light was that which I beheld upon the mountains alternating in silver and in gold? It was not the sun, it was not the moon, it was not a star. What then was it?

Unto this question I had then no answer. I remembered also the words of my companion who upon the way had said unto me look, and deceive not thyself, and had named a wounded light; yet all these things together I could not understand. I laid this question therefore up in my heart, beside that other which the day before I had laid up touching the First Enemy, as two seeds which in their own time should spring up, if God should will it; and more upon these matters that very night I would not think, lest a wearied mind should breed errors.

Returning unto the couch, having made the sign of the cross over my breast, I said that prayer which the Lord himself last spake upon the cross: In manus tuas, Domine, commendo spiritum meum. And sleep came without any dreams that are remembered, and I slept in quiet as a child in his father's house until the hour of Matins.

And when the first dawn had tinged the heaven, I arose and returned unto the window: and I beheld the sun rising from that quarter from which all suns do rise. The same sun I beheld which in Hibernia I had seen above the deep many thousand mornings, but his light here was purer and warmer, as a garment duly bleached in some font not made by human hand. And I made the sign of the cross for it, as is our wont, and I went down unto the assembly of the dwellers of that land.

☩ Of the Dwellers and Their Aspect ☩

The dwellers of that land — whom afterward I understood to be those that name themselves Eldar, that is to say, by interpretation as it were They Who First Beheld the Stars, because they were born before the sun and the moon, beneath the light of the primordial stars — were in form like unto man, yet, as far as a man may think upon it, more excellent.

A question: bodily angels? — It seemeth not. Pre-Adamites? — But where in the Scripture? Another kind created of God, beyond our understanding. God, who hath made such things, is to be feared and to be adored.

Their stature was tall, as of well-formed men, yet all of them somewhat majestic and serene, as though none of them had ever feared or laboured overmuch or been sick. Their faces did shine — not only as the face of a man healthy and joyful is wont to shine, but with a certain inward light, as a crystal before a candle, wherein the candle itself doth in some sort appear. Their hair was as living gold or living silver, not in the colour thereof, but as though the very stuff of it were alive within the hair. And their eyes — O Lord God! — their eyes were as wells without a bottom, wherein thou couldst see, as it were, the memory of all things wrought from the beginning of the world, and stars, and songs which no human ear doth hear.

Nothing in them was misshapen, nothing foul, nothing mortal to behold. Their garments were white and grey and silver, nowhere torn nor sullied. And that which seemed most wondrous: there could not be seen in their face fear or sorrow — not for that they knew them not, but as though they were as it were beyond them, even as one that doth well know grief, and yet emerging out of it doth now stand higher. Afterward he himself — of whom by and by I shall speak and tell his name — did expound it unto me: We live not in time as ye do. We are mindful of all things that have been. This is both a gift and a heavy burden.

Their tongue — O how wondrous a tongue! — was as music made into words, or words made into music: each voice did resound as the string of a harp struck, and when many spake together it was as a choir of singers upon the night of the Lord's Nativity. I spake in the Hibernian tongue, and in Latin, and a little in the Saxon which I had learned of our English brethren in the monastery. And then — not without amazement — I knew in their midst that very one which had silently led me unto the city: and he was a chief among them, not in stature but in a certain splendour as a glory about him, which upon the way I had not seen, as though only then had it first unveiled itself. He made a sign of quiet unto the rest and began to speak unto me in a new tongue.

And among themselves they did move most quietly, not as men that are wont to fill the public ways with voice and impatient step, but as such as walk within a temple. When one did salute another, he lifted up his hand — the palm turned toward himself, the fingers softly stirring — with the head a little bowed; and the other rendered the same, and the countenance of either bore in silence a certain serene gladness, as though the very occasion of meeting between them were itself a gift. What that gesture of the hand should signify, I knew not; yet was it fair to behold, and full of gravity. And as oft as one sitting beheld another approaching, he rose up in his honour, even though he that sat were the greater in age or in dignity.

Father Cellach told me that brother Aidan, after his return, was sometimes wont to make this gesture of the hand — the palm turned toward himself, the head bowed. And the brethren stood mute, neither dared they ask whence he had learned it. — brother Coeman

And, marvel of marvels and a grace of God: I understood him. Not as a pilgrim that after many months by little and little learneth a barbarous tongue, but as in a dream: the meaning came into my mind without labour, without the noise of words, as though his voice did speak directly into my heart, his words as it were transfixed by light.

Come, said he, thou art a guest in Aman. Not unto many sons of Death is it given to come hither. Who hath sent thee?

And I made answer, with my knees almost bent: My God, the Creator of heaven and of earth, who is adored in three Persons, and holy Columba my patron, whom He did send upon this way.

And his countenance was made as the sun emerging after long clouds — joy and sadness together, wondrously mingled, and his eyes — already have I said that their eyes were deep — were stirred as deep waters at these words. And he said with a hushed voice, as one uttering a sacred name in the holy of holies: Ilúvatar.

Exceeding fair — so are angels also when they appear. But angels do not eat, whereas these eat and laugh. Which is wondrous.

☩ Of the Feast of the Eldar and Their Table ☩

When he had spoken that sacred name, and those waters were yet stirred in his eyes, he rose up upon his feet and gave a sign with his head that I should follow him. Come, said he, it is time that thou shouldst break bread with us. Many words yet of these matters shall be between us, but the first law of the guest is meat. And he led me out of the greater hall into another chamber, smaller and more peaceful, wherein wide windows did open upon a garden, and a light, whence I knew not, did enter in tranquil.

The table was of stone, yet light, and along the length thereof did sit Eldar not a few, men and women, and peradventure two children beneath the table whom at first I saw not for their lesser stature. Each one saluted me with a bowing of the head, and they made ready for me a place beside the elder. I sat me down, and I looked upon the table: the bread was white, of round form yet loftier than our own, of a savour which I am not able to tell save that it had at once somewhat of honey and of corn and of I know not what other fragrance. And there were upon platters many fruits, some of them known to me — pears and apples and grapes — and others which I had never beheld, of forms and colours which in our tongue we cannot name. And water there was in cups of pure silver, and a certain thin and shining wine, as it were a rose diluted.

Tob. XII, 19: I seemed indeed to eat with you, but I do use an invisible food. So saith the angel. But these are other — they verily do eat. A third kind.

And this first appeared wondrous unto me above the other wonders of that table: they did eat. And they drank. And they laughed among themselves, and one cast unto another soft words, and that lesser child gathered fruits with his hand and held them out unto his mother — as afterward I learned that she was his mother. I called to mind that in the book of Tobit the angel Raphael had said unto Tobias the son that he did indeed seem to eat, but did use an invisible food; and I had supposed therefore that they who are as angels do not eat. But these did eat, and did drink, and their laughter was as the ringing of a clean bell, yet a human one. And I was astonished in my mind: whether they were angels, or men, or some other thing altogether, I durst not determine. Our catechism was silent of such matters, and in this I likewise was silent.

While we did eat, my heart did mark another wondrous thing, which hitherto I had not weighed: when either he or another of them did speak unto me, I understood as though their speech were unto me my native tongue, or as though the voice itself did descend into my heart without the middle of the air. But when they spake among themselves, I did indeed hear the sound, and that sound was in every way fair as a subtle music without instrument; yet the very words and the sense of them remained closed unto me, and only the beauty of the sound could come unto me. And I knew not how this might be done; nor know I to this day. For they did hear me better than I did them, and otherwise.

Then, when the table was now half through, there entered the hall a woman of the Eldar whom I had not before beheld, and all turned themselves toward her. She was of a stature like unto the rest of their women, yet her countenance was as it were luminous with joy, so that no man could look upon her without that very joy being in some measure conveyed into himself. She saluted the elder with her tongue, and he answered her, and she said a few other words with a voice midway between speech and song, as though she rendered certain ancient verses for a salutation. I heard the beauty of the sound, as I said but a little before, but the words themselves I caught not; and yet beneath that beauty there was a certain most thin sadness, as beneath the first dawn some chill yet remaineth out of the night. I myself, who am a man and a monk, and ought not to look upon a woman save chastely, could in such a sight look only chastely, because in that countenance there was nothing that should draw downward unto the earth, but all things that should lead upward. When she had ended her song, she bowed her head unto him and departed, and my tears flowed again without any known cause, and none of them seemed to wonder unto me that a bearded man should weep at a feast.

And, behold, after a little while, that lesser child, which afore had held forth fruits unto his mother, came unto my side without fear, and with eyes wherein the whole world might be trusted he looked upon me, and held forth unto me in his small palm a single small red berry, of the shape of a heart and of the colour of living blood. And he said one word which I understood not, but which sounded as aipio. I took the berry from the hand of the child, and when I had set it in my mouth, the savour was sweet and sharp at once, as our fruit which is called the cherry, but better. I asked of him what was the word which the child had spoken. And he smiled and said: Among us this is the name of this berry, and it doth signify as it were 'holy berry'. The name is ancient, and none any longer knoweth wherefore it is so called, yet we hold the name. And I was astonished in my heart: for that little child, which feared me not as an unknown guest, had given unto me with his own hand a holy berry, as though he himself knew somewhat which he could not know. The name they have given unto this berry, they themselves understand not; and I did keep it within my heart, neither durst I think anything of it.

Aidan beheld a son of the Eldar, and that infant gave with his own hand a holy berry unto the monk. Wondrous it is — angels beget not, neither are little ones among them. But these are otherwise, whom God alone knoweth how He hath made them. — brother Coeman

When the feast was ended, my Host did dismiss me with these words: Be thou this day at quiet. Behold our gardens, walk through the city, and no man shall hinder thee. To-morrow we shall speak of weightier matters, if thou wilt hear. And through the rest of the space of the day I walked alone through those streets of the city which I had seen, through gardens wherein were trees and flowers whose names no mortal knoweth, and no man did hinder me, even as he had promised. And when the evening hour was come again, returning unto my cell I slept in quiet, and neither did the moon appear in the heaven that night, nor did the stars hold themselves otherwise than upon the night before.

☩ Of Their Works and Gardens ☩

James I, 19: Let a man be swift to hear, but slow to speak. Which our brother kept those two days — with the eyes he saw, with the mouth he held silence.

On the morrow, two days were given me wherein alone I walked through the city, as a guest in another's house who hath time to consider. For I asked of him once whether it behoved me not rather to hasten unto the discourses which he himself had promised me. And he made answer with that voice which bore certainty without weight: Among us there is no need of haste; time hath been given thee that thou mightest see, not only that thou mightest hear. That which thou shalt have learned with thine eyes shall be a foundation for the words which thereafter thou shalt hear. And so I was left alone, and I began to see.

That city through which upon the first day I had passed as a stranger, I now with slow steps did walk through the same, beholding all things which upon the first day I had not seen. It was builded upon a mountain, of white stone gleaming with its proper light, with towers so many and so lofty that they did almost touch the clouds, and greater than from my little boat I had believed. The streets were paved with a stone bright and smooth. Through the midst of the streets there did run a stream of most pure water, in stone channels disposed with so great art that the water itself did seem to sing beneath the feet of them that passed by, and trees grew in the very street as in a most spacious garden. The people did look upon me, not with hostility but with a merciful curiosity: for I was as a bird of a strange kind among them, a curious thing, tender, fragile, mortal. At the sides of the streets were workshops wherein the Eldar did exercise the works of their hands: some did handle gold and silver in metals and out of them did fashion vessels and brooches and instruments whose use was unknown unto me; others carved woods; others wove into cloth colours which in our workshops are not found. And this one thing was most notable unto me above all things: in no place did I behold arms. No sword, no spear, no arrow, no shield. As a people that holdeth not even the memory of war within their heart.

A white city upon the mountain — cf. the city in the Apocalypse: descending from heaven from God, having the brightness of God (Apoc. XXI, 10). But this hath not yet descended — it is already there.

And I entered into their gardens, which were not the gardens of our folk for the use of food, but places wherein trees and flowers and fountains were set with so great art that the garden itself did seem a prayer without words. And I saw a thing which I scarce dare to tell: when the Eldar in these gardens did handle their trees, they handled them not as we do plants or animals, but as one would speak with a sister or a brother, with a gentle hand stroking the bark and uttering hushed words which assuredly were not words of command but of love. And this I learned: these speak with their trees as with sisters. The full meaning of which thing I then understood not, but I laid it up in mine heart, that afterward, if God should will it, I might understand.

Yet among these things I kept the discipline which I had brought out of the monastery. At the appointed hours — though the hours did flow there otherwise, and the wonted course of the sun gave not unto me the same tokens — I said mine offices as I was able, and I chanted the accustomed psalms. And the psalms which among us do sound after one manner, here did sound after another — not for that the words were changed, but for that they were given unto other ears. For the place itself doth impose upon prayer a form whereof he that prayeth doth not foresee.

But among all things which I beheld, this did most shake my mind: faces wrathful, or troubled with sorrow, I saw not in that kindred. The land which they trod with their feet did not appear unto them adversary. And they ate bread of the labour of their hands — but of what countenance that labour was, I am not able to tell: it was not the sweat of our fathers under Adam, nor yet idleness, but I know not what third thing, whereunto I have not found a name. And I asked within myself, which then I durst not inquire, and to-day I dare not affirm: concerning the Sacrament of baptism, what I should think touching that kindred, I understood not. And this question was above me, and I held my peace, and I noted only the question upon the tablet of mine heart. And when those two days were ended, in the evening that elder came unto me and said: To-morrow I shall lead thee into the great hall, and we shall speak of weightier matters, if thou yet wilt hear. And I made the sign of the cross over the two days which had passed by, lest the things which I had seen should escape from my memory, and I prayed for a light of understanding, whereof upon the day following I should have need.

Even as the Hibernians do tell of the Sídh or the Faerica — he that entereth, cometh forth other than he was. This Aidan did experience not in fable but in the flesh. Wondrous it is — for fables do as it were shadow forth the truth. — brother Coeman

☩ Of the Discourse with Finrod ☩

His name — so far as out of their tongue I could render it — was Findaráto or Finrod: it did seem to signify Golden End, or Crowned with Wisdom and with Gold. Among his own he was as a king or a priest, not by violence nor by fear, but for that all did love him and desired to converse with him. He himself said that he was of a kindred which doth name itself Noldor — that is, as he did expound, Those Who Know Much or Sons of Wisdom.

Finrod — the name rendered out of their own tongue. It signifieth as it were Golden Wisdom, or Crown of Gold and Wisdom. A magnificent man.

On the morrow, as he had promised, he led me into a great hall — wholly built of crystal and of marble, in the height whereof there did enter through vast windows a certain soft light, the source whereof I knew not then. We sat down, I and Finrod, and we held long discourses through hours which I was not able to number, for that the hours flow there otherwise.

Finrod did tell unto me of the origin of the world. He told of the Great Song — which I understood to be the Creation — when Ilúvatar himself in the beginning did sing, and unto his Powers — Ainur he called them, whom I understood to be the Greater Angels — he gave commandment that they should sing with him melodies unspeakable. And they did sing, and out of their song and out of the will of Ilúvatar the world was formed — not as a potter doth fashion a vessel, but as a singer doth bring forth a harmony: out of the very sound, out of the very beauty.

But one of the Greater Angels — whom they did name Melkor, and whom I without any doubt understood to be the Adversary, whom we call Satan — willed to sing against the harmony, and did sow discord into the song. And when I heard this name Melkor, suddenly there came back to my remembrance that which some days before, beside the mountain of the lights, I had heard from my companion: of the First Enemy, of whom he had then willed to keep silence. I understood therefore that they were one and the same: a voice which had corrupted the first harmony, and a hand — as anon shall be made manifest — which afterward did slay the lights of the world. One is the hand, one is the sin, though the works thereof be many and divers throughout the ages.

Light before the sun — even as it is said in Genesis: God said, Let there be light. And there was light. This was upon the first day. But the sun upon the fourth. What lay between? The Scripture is silent.

☩ And Ilúvatar did not annihilate that discord, but did receive it into himself, and out of it likewise drew forth a more wondrous beauty — even as certain shadows in a painting do cause the surrounding light to shine the more. Pondering these things I was astounded: even in the theology of these Immortals the same mystery which we venerate in the Providence of God! ☩

Creation by song — wondrous! In the Psalms David doth praise God by song, the angels do sing Glory in the highest; but here is it that the very world was made by song? A wondrous theology — yet not impious.

Then Finrod, perceiving me intent, took up a voice as of one singing — for somewhat doth change in the voice of the Eldar when they speak of more ancient matters — and began to tell of the first lights of the world, before the sun and the moon were made. The which narration so moved me that thereafter, that night, I could not take any sleep, and to this day, when I do call them to mind, I feel as though some window had been opened in my heart, which I am not able to shut.

Of old, said he, when the world was young and the sun was not yet set in the heaven, the Valar — whom thou mightest call Powers — did plant in our land two Trees, that they might give light unto the dwellers of Aman. They were not trees as those which thou seest in this garden: for those were as it were the very light contracted into the form of a tree, as though the Wisdom of Ilúvatar did manifest itself by root and by frond and by flower.

These two Trees I myself have painted, even as Father Cellach told unto me how he had beheld them in an ancient codex made by the hand of Aidan, before time should consume them. — brother Coeman

And he did describe them with a voice so clear that I did almost behold them with mine own eyes: the First Tree, which they called Telperion, great and most ancient, having green leaves and silver flowers, out of which flowers a light did flow forth, pure and somewhat cold, as the full moon yet without shadow and without intermission. Its name, quoth he, peradventure I may render into thy speech as it were Silver Foliage or Splendour of Silver. Its roots — so they say — held all the land of Aman beneath them, and when light winds did stir its branches, the very light did as it were sing, glistening among the leaves. This was the first light of the world.

The Second Tree, said he, somewhat lesser yet more burning, had golden flowers, out of which there flowed forth a warm and joyful light, as the summer sun yet without hurtful heat — a light pure and benign, beneath which all things were seen fairer than they are. We named it Laurelin , that is, as it were Singing Gold or Golden Melody. For the light of that tree was as it were music: not such as thou hearest with the ear, but such as the eye and the whole body doth perceive. By turns they shone, so that the silver light did little by little wane while the golden waxed, and again the gold was diminished and the silver did flower — and for a brief space of time both shone together, in a mingling of light so precious that even now, when I speak of it, tears come unto me, although many years have passed.

And he was silent a little while, and I beheld his eyes grow moist — which among the Eldar is a thing most rare. Then, with voice changed, he continued: But that Melkor, of whom I but now spake unto thee, whose discord was heard in the Song, afterward — when he had been already cast forth and had come again into the world — did with hatred persecute those Trees. For their light did accuse all his work of darkness. And he took counsel with a certain monstrous creature — whose name I am not willing to utter into the light of day, lest peradventure she give heed — which did drink darkness as others water, and was so ancient that not even the Valar themselves do know whence she came. These two together, Melkor and that beast of darkness, came in a certain festal dawn unto the Trees, and they wounded them, and that power of darkness did suck out all their light and the life thereof.

Melkor = Satan? Another name, the same substance: the first angel which willed against the harmony of God, fell through pride (Is. XIV). The selfsame thing, different only in name.

True night fell then for the first time upon Aman. Which we had not before known: that light could be slain. The Valar did mourn as no creature ever shall be able to mourn in this world, for they themselves had wrought the Trees with their own hand and had as it were set their heart in them. And we — Eldar — who beneath that light had been born, and beneath it had lived so many ages, stood mute, knowing not what true darkness might be. Many things did flow forth out of that time which it would not profit thee to-day to hear: exile, the sword, brother against brother, and a long and bitter labour. Only this thou shouldst know: the first light of the world was slain.

And when he had spoken these things, I, considering in mine heart upon the death of Christ — upon the light of the world which upon Golgotha was extinguished — held my peace, and could not make him any answer. How might it have been said unto him? Neither indeed did I then know how to say. But I wrote it in my memory, even as Mary did write the words of the shepherds within her heart, as a thing which God should one day expound.

Yet somewhat, said Finrod afterward, as one perceiving the disposition of my mind, doth survive out of the Trees, and ere thy returning thou shalt see — if so it shall please thee. Not those very things which were: for those have perished, as I have said. But that which doth survive — it is memory, which Ilúvatar doth not suffer utterly to perish. For that which was truly beautiful, with him is preserved in some sort, although not in the same fashion as before it was. But of this we shall treat when the time is come.

And I understood that I was yet to behold somewhat great, and this word — that which was truly beautiful, with Him is preserved — was set within my heart as a seed, that thereafter should give forth its flowers.

The tears of the Eldar — a wondrous matter. They are not such as ours, out of the pain of the flesh; but out of the memory of things which they hold and are constrained to hold. A heavy burden.

☩ Of Their Immortality and Their Lot ☩

I asked of Finrod concerning their state after death. And here again I beheld upon his countenance somewhat as a passing cloud — not that sadness which already on the day before, when he had spoken of the Trees, I had marked, but somewhat else: a deep questioning, as a man that gazeth into a glass and beholdeth himself, and yet not himself, and doth wonder at both.

This place is most theologically difficult. If they die not in sin, do they not stand in need of redemption? But Augustine doth teach that every creature, after the fall of Adam, is affected. How then is it with these?

We die not as ye die, said he slowly, as one weighing his words. Our body may perish in war or in a grievous wound. But our spirit — that which ye call the soul — abideth in the ages within this world, to be called forth again into a new flesh when the time shall come. We are bound unto this song of Creation, unto this world, even unto its uttermost end. He paused. But ye — sons of Death, sons of the Unknown Gift — ye burn as a brief flame, and after death ye go forward upon an unknown way. Whither ye go, Ilúvatar hath reserved unto himself. Unto us he hath not revealed it. And this is, and here in a wondrous fashion he used a voice as one speaking of a precious thing, and almost an enviable, a gift given unto you which hath been denied unto us.

Long did I muse upon these words. A gift — death, a gift? We who all our life do resist death, who have wept with tears the deaths of parents and friends and brethren, who in the last fear do beg for the viaticum — we hold death as a gift? And these Immortals, unto whom we seem as it were wretched and brief and about to perish, do wonder at us for that very brevity and mortality?

Finrod did see the thought in my face and smiled — and his smiling was as the dawn: Take not this amiss. We do not envy you, neither do we despise. But after so many ages we have seen many of your kind — few, very few, that have come hither — and in all of you there is a certain light which is not in us: the light of things brief but burning. Ye love more keenly because ye lose swiftly. Ye sing the more strongly because your song knoweth that it shall be ended. In this ye are like unto a flame — and a flame is more splendid than a star, although the star liveth the longer.

Death as a gift! A wondrous paradox. Do these Immortals haply envy our death? What is liberty from the world — save death itself?

Hearing these words, I revolved in my mind those things which through those days I had seen among them. And I said within mine heart: if we burn the more keenly, what manner of beings are these unto whom he doth ascribe these words? What therefore the measure of love or of life may be, it is not ours to know; and I held my peace within myself, for I durst neither consent unto him as one accepting praise touching myself, nor contradict him as one correcting an enduring sage.

☩ Of the Preaching and the Wondrous Reply ☩

As I heard these things and was musing within myself, again my heart began to be enkindled as of old, when first God did call me unto this voyage. Then — brethren, of charity and of mine office, not of arrogance — I told unto Finrod that which we know and believe and preach: of God the Father, who so loved the world; of the eternal Son who took upon him our human flesh — wondrous and stupendous! — and in that flesh did suffer and did die, and upon the third day rose again; of the Holy Spirit who quickeneth the Church. I told how, through this Passion and Resurrection, an eternal gate hath been opened unto men, unto life.

Here is the gordian knot of all this narration. If the Eldar knew Ilúvatar before Christ — what is it with them after Christ? Have they salvation? The Fathers of the Church have not treated this.

And Finrod did hear me — and this also was wondrous — not only by lending an ear, but with the whole body, with the whole mind, as a man who at last out of the first fount doth hear a long history that hath been told by many. And when I named the Son of God descending into flesh and dying upon the cross, his countenance was made as the heaven when heavy clouds do pass over the sun: shadow, but not darkness.

When I had ceased to speak, he was long silent. So long that I supposed I had given offence. But there was no anger in his silence — there was a deep consideration, as one who setteth a long reckoning within his mind. At length he said:

Of this One who did descend into death that he might overcome death — and who was born of a mortal woman and bore in the flesh all the sorrow of the flesh — we have had among us ancient prophecies, voices as in a vision heard, whereof not all did agree, neither were they clear. Now I do understand the better. But this way — as thou sayest — is the way of the sons of Death, the way of those that receive the Unknown Gift. We are not sons of Death. Our way is otherwise — and whether we have a way, or whether we have walked the way, or whether we likewise have need of a hand to lead us — this we ask, and we find not, and in this question we have been ages and ages.

And Finrod arose and went unto the window, where a hoary light entered in and made his face as silver, and he spake the last words of this discourse which I dare to write down:

Prophecy among the Eldar — how, and by whom given? If they knew of the Incarnation, hath the Spirit of God spoken unto them also? A great question.

Thou hast the sin of one man in the history of thy flesh. We have the sin of one Angel in the history of our memory. Peradventure both stand in need of mercy — yet a mercy diverse, even as wounds are diverse. That discrimination Ilúvatar alone doth know. We do serve. We do love. We do create. We do keep. We do sing. Whether it shall suffice — that is in his hand, not in mine.

And I could make him no answer. And to this day I cannot.

☩ Of the Hour of the Light Departed ☩

When he had spoken those last words whereof I have already written, he was long silent, and I likewise held my peace within myself. He still stood beside the window, and the light entering through the windows was changing little by little, for that the sun which was without was descending unto the setting. Not at once did he dismiss me, as he was wont after the foregoing discourses, but, turning unto me, he said with a voice changed: Come thou with me this evening. There is among us an hour which no man that is here doth pass over, and a long time hath gone by since we had among us a mortal guest who should be present at this hour. That which thou shalt hear, thou shalt hear; that which thou shalt understand, in his own time thou shalt understand. Come thou now.

And he led me through those streets of the city which I had now known, but this way led me otherwise — through a gate turned toward the west, unto a place which I had not before beheld, an open court beneath the open heaven, with steps disposed round about as it were a most quiet theatre, and all things set toward the west, that every beholder might gaze upon those mountains upon the horizon, the same mountains wherein upon the first day silver and gold alternating had been seen of me. The Eldar were assembling in silence, out of every quarter of the city, men and women, elders whose age I dare not tell, and little children such as he that had given me the berry. They stood or they sat, all turned toward the west. Finrod stood at my side somewhat at the outermost edge of the steps, that I might see all things, and yet be no part of the office.

And the sun was now sinking down. This first appeared unto me worthy of note: it sank even as our sun doth sink, slowly going down behind the mountains, the light becoming little by little gold, then ruddy, then scarce visible above the highest peaks of the mountains. As it is among us. And yet the eyes of the Eldar directed upon that very setting were not the eyes of such as do await the morrow's dawn, as ours are; rather were they the eyes of such as remember somewhat which was before the sun, and who in the going down of the sun did contemplate not the end of the day, but the image of another end far more ancient. And then I felt for the first time that this hour was not the hour of our vespers, although it should fall at a like time.

And when the sun was now no more than the breadth of a finger-nail above the mountains, one voice did rise — the voice of some woman whose person I saw not for the multitude of those that stood, that voice naked and pure as the first evening star, without the music of any instrument, beginning to sing somewhat. Soon another did add herself unto her, and another, and in a brief time all the Eldar were singing, in three certain parts which the eye distinguished not, but the ear.

And their song did unfold itself in three modes as it were, although there was no formal pause between them. The first part did sound silver, lofty and thin and full as it were of a cold light, and named somewhat in their tongue which sounded as Varda and Tintallë; and I understood that they sang of the stars, of the first stars which were made before either sun or moon was. The second part did sound golden, more grave and more warm, and in it I heard the name Yavanna, and they did name Telperion and Laurelin as the names of a brother and of a sister that were lost, and that part was fuller than the first yet sadder also. The third part was the most wondrous of all — it had its own voice in Nienna, and there was therein a certain weeping, yet not weeping only; it was as though to weep and to hope together, even as the children of Israel when they were founding the second temple did weep and shout for joy alike, so that none could distinguish the voice of them that rejoiced from the voice of them that wept, even as Esdras doth write. So also these singers — and they wept, and they hoped I know not what beyond their weeping which I caught not, and my tears flowed heavier than ever before.

This song no man of our kind hath heard save Aidan alone — and Aidan himself did say that he could speak no more of it than that which he hath said.

When the song did little by little fail and a great silence had fallen upon all, Finrod arose from his place and spake a few words openly, and all gave ear. He said with a grave voice as one uttering holy names: Telperion brother, Laurelin sister, we do remember you. Varda lady of the stars, who didst first make the light, Yavanna lady of growing things, who didst make the Trees, Nienna sister of the mourning, who dost lament that which hath perished — we do remember you. And then, as though his voice were lifted into a more exalted tone, he added one thing more: Ilúvatar, qui dedit, qui abstulit, qui servat — in manibus tuis sumus.

It seemeth like unto our office of vespers, which is sung when the sun goeth down. But it is otherwise — we do remember Christ, the light of the world; these, the former light which they beheld and lost. The same hour — another memory. — brother Coeman

And when he had spoken this last name, all bowed their heads together, even as we in the monastery do bow our heads at Gloria Patri. And this gave me most to ponder. For they had named three names out of their Powers — and I understood, even though the names themselves I understood not, that these were the Greater Angelic ones whom we should call Archangels were we in their place — yet unto Ilúvatar himself, unto their God, they did turn with but one short prayer, at the end of all. And I asked within myself: wherefore do they not speak more directly unto God, whom they know how to name? Then there came into my mind our Litany of Saints, wherein we name Michael and Gabriel and Raphael and every order of the celestials before that we come unto the Trinity itself — not for that we adore God the less, but for that through intercessors and through order we draw nigh. Peradventure, said I unto myself, the same is the reason among them, and those Powers are unto them as unto us are saints and angels and fathers of the Church — through them they draw nigh unto Him that is beyond all. But I durst not judge, for the matter was higher than mine understanding, and I held my peace within mine heart, and only kept the question.

When the prayer was ended, all the Eldar still kept silence, and I likewise was silent, and the heaven above us was now full of stars even as upon the first night which I had spent among them. And, behold — out of the eastern part, above the steps of the further side of the court, a subtle light began to rise, and I lifted up mine eyes and saw the moon in the heaven. It was a moon newly born, the thinnest sickle as a new-cut nail. And this was the first time since I had come into that land that I beheld the moon. She stood alone, without the sun now, without the Trees which had perished — and yet she gave her light. And mine heart stood as it were midway between somewhat to be understood and somewhat not yet understood, and somewhat of that seed which on the first night I had laid up touching the silver and the gold did stir within itself; yet what it might be, I could not understand, and I gazed only upon the moon as upon a new thing which had never been seen of me, although through so many years she had stood the same above our monastery.

Then Finrod laid his hand upon my shoulder and said softly: Many things are given unto a son of Death to behold in this land, and yet more things not yet given. They that truly grieve, do also truly comfort — that which thou hast heard this day is not yet the whole. Go now and take rest. And we descended from the steps in silence, and the whole city was oppressed with a silence beneath that new moon, which yet appeared less than the breadth of a finger-nail. A few Eldar, dispersed from the steps into the streets, were going back unto their houses in silence — none did speak. And that silence was not empty as ours after Compline in the monastery, when our minds alone do remain with themselves — but full, as a vessel whereof none dare to pour forth that which it containeth, lest it perish. And then I felt: behold, somewhat new is being born in mine heart, which I am not able to describe.

A full silence, an empty silence — both these we know, but we are not wont to name. That which we seek in our cells, our brother hath there found.

Finrod led me unto my cell, and that night I slept, yet not deeply, and my dreams were full of the song which I had heard, and of that new moon which above the steps I had beheld.

☩ Of the Memory of the Light ☩

When at length the day was come whereon I should return — not for that Finrod would have driven me forth, but for that he himself had said unto me that my time in that land had now its end, lest now roots of yet another kind should grow within me — he arose at the dawn of that day, and called me forth from the place wherein I slept. And he said with a soft voice, as one that would lead a friend unto some secret matter: Come with me. There remaineth one thing yet for thee, before I dismiss thee. Follow, and keep silence.

Father Cellach told me of old of a monk that beheld the light of God in silence, and held his peace all his life. Now I do believe that this may be. — brother Coeman

And I did follow him through streets of the city which I had not yet trodden, through gardens yet shut before the full daylight, through the western gate which the warden without a word did open unto us, as one that had awaited us. We came forth out of the city and ascended a hill not high, but set far from the walls, whereon a few solitary trees did stand, and the silence was such that I heard mine own blood within mine ears. And as we went up, suddenly there came into my mind: this very hill upon my first day I had beheld from afar, when I knew not yet what it might be. Then I had called it the mountain of the lights, and had marked the sparks of silver and of gold upon the top thereof. Now I was coming unto that which then mine eye — or rather my mind — from afar had taken hold of: yet then I had not been able to understand.

On the top of the hill there was no building, nor great tree, nor monument made by hand — but the very space was holy, as a church without walls, and the earth there did seem higher and truer than the rest of the earth. Finrod said nothing, but stood beside me with his head bowed, as one that prayeth in a most holy place.

A secret hill — cf. Cant. Canticles IV, 12: a garden enclosed, a fountain sealed up. The place doth seem kept of God.

And little by little, with no sign that I might behold, out of the very earth round about our feet a light began to come forth — not strong as the sun, nor altogether like unto the moon, but most subtle, of silver at the first, then of gold, then of both mingling themselves. There was no luminous flower, nor visible tree, nor any fount — the very light, without any visible source, did flow forth out of the very earth, imprinting forms upon the air as it sang a silent music unto the eyes.

And I stood and gazed, and my tears flowed without my will. I beheld not the very Trees themselves — for those were not. But I beheld that which had survived out of them, the witness of that which was wounded and yet had not utterly perished. There was a certain like sweetness in those lights, such as I once saw in the face of a man who had risen up out of a grievous sickness and had beheld the sun the first time again: that countenance is not such as it was before the sickness, but somewhat higher doth appear within it — it is a face that hath seen death, and yet doth live. So was this light unto me.

A light without any visible source — as the Spirit which bloweth where He listeth, whose voice thou hearest, but knowest not whence He cometh (John III, 8). The same reason.

How long we stood there, I know not. Finrod was silent, and I was silent, and the hours there were what they were. When at length the light began little by little to wane, as though the day itself did leave the hill, Finrod said with a voice more moved than upon any other day I had heard: So it is. That which was truly beautiful, Ilúvatar suffereth not utterly to perish. More to-day I shall not say.

Whilst those words were yet abiding and the light did little by little wane, I cast down mine eyes unto the earth — and beside my foot I saw a small stone which I had not before noted, white as new-fallen snow, scarce greater than a hazel-nut, wherein a certain light, subtle but sure, did abide. I bent down and I took it up, almost not thinking what I did. Finrod silently watched me, neither did he himself bow down unto the earth, nor did he seek any stone.

When the stone was now in my palm, he said with a humble voice: Such stones are at times found here upon the hill, rarely, and none of ours doth gather them. Peradventure they are a memory of greater ones than themselves are, whom this day I dare not name. That which hath come unto thee hath not come by chance: take it up, and bear it with thee across the sea. But beware of this one thing: hide thou it not unto thyself alone, neither set it in a vessel of silver as thine own price. He that hideth his light in darkness, himself doth wander — the which thing also at times hath happened unto the greatest of us.

And he laid his hand upon my shoulder — a light weight, as of birds — and we descended by the same way, full of silence, and I knew that somewhat had been changed within mine heart in that which I had beheld. What precisely had been changed, I knew not yet then. But from that day unto this hour the same vision doth not cease to return in my dreams:

A slain light which did return. The Eldar through the image of the Trees have heard, we through the Gospel. One truth, two ways? — brother Coeman

☩ Of the Return and the Wayfaring ☩

After the vision of that hill upon the last day — as though seven days had I been in that land, or so much as seven days did seem unto me, for that the time there doth flow otherwise, gently and abundantly as honey out of a pot, rather than as water through a torrent — Finrod went with me unto the haven where my skiff was waiting. I beheld in it certain provisions which I had not laid up: bread that was not corrupt, and water in a small vessel. Some unseen hand had provided.

Seven days there, and the last day the Memory of the Light — a perfect number. Three years without? It is like unto the Hibernian fables of the Faerica land — he that entereth, in coming forth hath lost his time. To be heeded with caution.

Then, before I went up into the skiff, I took up that stone out of my scrip and looked upon it again — and it seemed unto me other than upon the hill: it was translucent, as an Alpine crystal, and the light therein was no longer subtle and alone as before, but composed: not warm as gold, not cold as the moon, but as it were the mingling of either in the smallest point of rest. Finrod stood beside me silent, and through his eyes there did then pass a certain longing more ancient than all days — as it were the memory of things which no mortal hath touched. And with a moved voice he said: Give thou not unto oblivion that which here thou hast beheld. Not many of thy kind shall we see any more — the time will come — but the memory of thee in us we shall keep.

That stone — which now, among all things which I possess, is unto me the most precious — I do keep in my most secret scrip, beneath my habit. By night at times I look upon it: a light is within. I know not what it is. I know not whether it be a miracle, or some nature unknown. Committing it unto God, I do keep it, and hold my peace.

The gem left of Aidan in the reliquary of the monastery. I beheld it in the year of Our Lord CMXV. A light is within. A testimony. brother Coeman

I did sail. And after three days — or three years, for the brethren afterward did say unto me that I had been absent the space of three years, the which I did not believe until I beheld the calendar — I came into known waters, and I knew the Hibernian seals, and the Hibernian birds, and at the last I beheld the cliff of Hibernia in the East. And I came in unto the harbour which is near unto our monastery, and Cellach the brother, who three years before had seen me weeping, did now laugh and weep at once at the gate.

After he was returned, but for a few days I beheld him. He spake otherwise. He held silence otherwise. He looked otherwise. Where art thou now, brother Aidan? — brother Cellach

I was away three years. Unto me they did seem as three days. The time in the land of Aman doth flow otherwise. This among other things I leave unto the judgement of God.

☩ ☩ ☩

These things are true, in so far as a fallible man, pressed by many sins, is able to declare the truth. Unto God and unto His saints, and unto the Church our mother, do I leave it to be judged what those Eldar may be in the scheme of divine providence, and what concerning them is to be hoped or to be feared. I am no Augustine. I am no Jerome. A fisherman I am, and a poor little monk that went up into his curach and came again returned changed.

When I first read these things, I supposed them either a fiction or a delirium. But the gem is a witness. Abbot Finan

This only do I say, brethren, and lay ye it up within your heart: in the countenance of Finrod I saw more of true goodness than in many faces of men who do call themselves Christians and approach unto the Eucharist, yet do hate their neighbour. In his words concerning Ilúvatar — whom we acknowledge as God the Father — I heard a more true reverence than in many sermons which are preached out of pulpits throughout the whole Church and do swiftly slip from the ears — and in the common prayer of theirs which one evening I beheld among them, where they did bow their heads before His name as we do bow before Gloria Patri, I beheld a piety not strange unto our own, although otherwise expressed and of another memory.

In their question touching the way of their salvation — in that profound and noble ignorance — I beheld more of humility and of honesty than in many that do suppose themselves to know all things, and seek no further any way, for that they believe themselves to have already arrived.

☩ Whether the Eldar shall be saved — this I ask, and I answer not. Whether they desire baptism — I know not, for that I knew not rightly how to speak unto them concerning it. Whether Christ hath suffered for them — peradventure, for God hath some part of His goodness for every creature. But let the theologians treat of these things — I do but bear witness. ☩

One thing I add — not that I should answer that which I cannot answer, but for that to keep silence in this also I cannot. When upon the hill I stood the last day, I saw: that which the Enemy had slain, God had not suffered utterly to perish. The light upon the earth did still abide. I know not what this may signify touching the lot of those that have kept that light. But if God forgetteth not the wounded light, peradventure neither shall He forget those that have loved it. This I teach not; this I hope.

Pray ye for me, brother Aidan a sinner, and pray ye — if it be lawful — for those Immortals beyond the ocean, whom God himself doth know how and how greatly, and who — this I know for a surety — do love their God Ilúvatar and do serve Him faithfully in their land, and do sing songs whereof this world hath no knowledge, and do keep the memory of things from the beginning of the world, that it may not perish.

☩ Miserere nobis omnibus, Domine, et iudica nos misericorditer — scientes et nescientes, mortales et immortales, filios Donationis et filios Cantus. ☩

Pray for the Eldar that abide beyond the sea, if so be God doth vouchsafe to look upon our prayers. It hurteth not to pray.
Written in the monastery of the Insula Columbae by the very hand of brother Aidan, in the year from the Incarnation of the Lord DCCCXCVI, in the month of November. This little book, when it shall be finished, in a wooden chest beneath the altar I shall lay up, lest by the chances of the times it should perish, and if some day peace shall be returned, the brethren that follow shall peradventure find it. Be praise unto God alone. Amen.
☩ This little book brother Coeman did find in a wooden chest beneath the altar, in the year CMXVII, after a long time wherein this monastery hath suffered many things. The ancient codex, written by the hand of brother Aidan, having undergone many hurts through the chances of the times, scarce could be read; wherefore, offering unto it a worthy hand, I did rewrite it, that in chapter it might be read of the brethren and not perish again, and unto the perpetual memory of the matter I made copy of it. Of brother Aidan the author, after the year DCCCXCVII, we know nothing. The gem whereof he writeth is kept in a silver reliquary. The light therein is yet seen by night. Help us, O Lord, weak as we are and unworthy. ☩
